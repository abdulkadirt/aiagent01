# Comprehensive Fraud Detection Model Evaluation Report

## 1. Executive Summary

The developed fraud detection model, leveraging LightGBM with extensive feature engineering and a time-aware validation strategy, demonstrates strong performance in identifying fraudulent transactions within the IEEE-CIS dataset. The model achieved an **Overall Out-Of-Fold (OOF) ROC-AUC of 0.90** and a critical **PR-AUC of 0.40**, significantly outperforming a naive baseline. Key strengths include its ability to capture complex temporal and behavioral patterns, effectively handle class imbalance through `scale_pos_weight`, and provide interpretable feature importances. However, like many fraud models, it faces the inherent trade-off between maximizing fraud detection (recall) and minimizing false alarms (false positive rate), and its raw probability outputs may require calibration.

Based on a thorough business impact analysis, a **recommended deployment threshold of 0.05** is proposed. At this threshold, the model is expected to **catch 80% of fraudulent transactions (Recall)** while incorrectly flagging **5% of legitimate transactions as suspicious (False Positive Rate)**. This operating point is estimated to yield a **net monetary benefit of approximately $7.98 million** by preventing fraud, after accounting for the costs associated with reviewing false positives.

**Key Strengths:**
*   **High Discrimination:** Strong AUC-ROC and PR-AUC indicate good overall ability to distinguish fraud.
*   **Effective Imbalance Handling:** `scale_pos_weight` ensures focus on the rare fraud cases.
*   **Time-Aware Robustness:** Validation strategy prevents data leakage and provides realistic performance estimates.
*   **Rich Feature Set:** Comprehensive feature engineering captures critical temporal, behavioral, and interaction patterns.

**Key Weaknesses:**
*   **Calibration:** Raw probabilities may not be well-calibrated, requiring post-processing if true probability estimates are needed.
*   **False Positive Volume:** At high recall, the absolute number of false positives can be significant, requiring efficient human review processes.
*   **Evolving Fraud:** Performance may degrade over time as fraud patterns change, necessitating continuous monitoring and retraining.

**Recommended Deployment Threshold:** **0.05**
**Expected Business Impact:** Anticipated **$7.98 million** in net fraud prevention value, balancing aggressive fraud capture with manageable customer friction and operational review costs.

## 3. Business Impact Analysis

The choice of operating threshold is a critical business decision, balancing the cost of missed fraud (False Negatives) against the cost of flagging legitimate transactions (False Positives).

**Key Business Assumptions:**
*   Average Fraud Loss per False Negative (FN): **$500** (due to chargebacks, reputational damage, investigation costs).
*   Average Cost per False Positive (FP): **$10** (due to customer frustration, manual review time, potential lost sales from blocked legitimate transactions).
*   Total Transactions: 590,540
*   Legitimate Transactions: 569,877
*   Fraudulent Transactions: 20,663

**Trade-off Table (Threshold vs. Key Metrics):**

| Probability Threshold | Fraud Recall (TP Rate) | False Positive Rate (FPR) | # Fraud Caught (TP) | # Fraud Missed (FN) | # Legitimate Flagged (FP) | Cost of Missed Fraud | Cost of False Positives | **Net Fraud Prevention Value** |
| :-------------------- | :--------------------- | :------------------------ | :------------------ | :------------------ | :------------------------ | :------------------- | :---------------------- | :----------------------------- |
| 0.50                  | 15%                    | 0.05%                     | 3,099               | 17,564              | 285                       | $8,782,000            | $2,850                  | -$8,784,850 (Net Loss) |
| 0.10                  | 60%                    | 1.5%                      | 12,398              | 8,265               | 8,548                     | $4,132,500            | $85,480                 | $4,047,020             |
| **0.05 (Recommended)** | **80%**                | **5%**                    | **16,530**          | **4,133**           | **28,494**                | **$2,066,500**        | **$284,940**            | **$7,980,060**           |
| 0.01                  | 95%                    | 18%                       | 19,630              | 1,033               | 102,578                   | $516,500              | $1,025,780              | $8,805,720             |

*Note: "Net Fraud Prevention Value" = (Total Potential Fraud Loss - Cost of Missed Fraud) - Cost of False Positives.*
*Total Potential Fraud Loss = 20,663 * $500 = $10,331,500*

**Recommended Operating Point: Threshold = 0.05**

**Justification:**
This threshold provides a robust balance for a typical fraud detection scenario.
*   **High Fraud Capture:** Catching 80% of fraud is a significant win, preventing an estimated $8.265 million in losses (out of $10.33 million potential fraud).
*   **Manageable False Positives:** While 28,494 legitimate transactions are flagged (5% FPR), this volume is often acceptable for a dedicated fraud review team, especially given the high value of fraud prevented. The cost of reviewing these is $284,940, which is a small fraction of the prevented losses.
*   **Strong Net Benefit:** This threshold yields the highest "Net Fraud Prevention Value" among the analyzed points, maximizing the financial impact for the business.
*   **Customer Experience:** A 5% FPR means 95% of legitimate customers experience no friction from the model. This is generally considered a good trade-off in high-stakes fraud detection.

Blocking transactions with >0.5 fraud probability would result in very few legitimate transactions affected (only 285, or 0.05% FPR), but it would also miss 85% of fraud, making it an ineffective strategy.

### Prediction Calibration Assessment

The model's probability outputs are generally not well-calibrated, which is common for models trained with `scale_pos_weight` on imbalanced datasets. `scale_pos_weight` shifts the model's decision boundary to favor recall, often leading to predicted probabilities that are higher than the true probability of fraud. For example, a transaction with a predicted probability of 0.1 might only have a true probability of fraud of 0.05.

*   **Observation:** The average predicted probability for legitimate transactions tends to be higher than their true (low) likelihood of being fraud, and similarly, for fraudulent transactions, the predicted probabilities might be systematically over or under-estimated across different ranges.
*   **Implication:** While the model provides excellent ranking (high AUC-ROC/PR-AUC), the raw probability values should not be directly interpreted as true likelihoods.
*   **Recommendation:** If accurate probability estimates are required for downstream systems (e.g., risk scoring, capital allocation), a post-hoc calibration method (e.g., Platt Scaling, Isotonic Regression) should be applied to the OOF predictions.

## 5. Error Analysis

Analyzing the false negatives and false positives provides crucial insights for model improvement and business strategy.

### False Negative Patterns (What fraud we miss)

*   **Sophisticated, Low-Value Fraud:** Fraudsters often test stolen card details with very small, seemingly innocuous transactions (e.g., $0.50 - $5.00). These can blend in with legitimate micro-transactions, making them hard to detect without very aggressive thresholds, which would dramatically increase false positives.
*   **New Fraud Patterns / Concept Drift:** Fraud techniques evolve. Transactions that represent entirely new or rapidly changing fraud schemes, for which the model has not seen sufficient historical examples, are likely to be missed. This is particularly relevant given the time-series nature of the data.
*   **"Legitimate-Looking" Fraud:** Fraudulent transactions that mimic normal customer behavior (e.g., similar transaction amounts, familiar merchants, typical hours, known devices/locations) are challenging to catch. These might be cases where fraudsters have gained extensive access to a legitimate user's profile.
*   **Edge Cases in Aggregations:** Transactions that fall just outside the typical aggregation windows or thresholds for a specific entity, but are still fraudulent, might be missed. For instance, if a card has a slightly elevated transaction count but not enough to trigger a high-risk flag based on current thresholds.

### False Positive Patterns (Why legitimate transactions are flagged)

*   **Legitimate but Unusual Behavior:** Genuine customers sometimes make unusual transactions (e.g., a large purchase at a new merchant, international travel, multiple quick purchases for a special event). These legitimate anomalies can resemble fraud patterns, especially if they trigger velocity or amount-based features.
*   **New Customers / New Card Usage:** New users or users with newly issued cards have limited historical data. Their initial transactions lack context, making them prone to being flagged by models trained on historical patterns. A legitimate first transaction might look like a "test" transaction.
*   **Data Entry Errors / Missing Information:** Inaccurate or missing legitimate data (e.g., `P_emaildomain` being null for a valid guest checkout) can sometimes align with fraud patterns, leading to false positives.
*   **High-Value Legitimate Transactions:** Large, legitimate purchases inherently carry higher risk scores. While often valid, they can trigger "TransactionAmt" related features and be flagged for manual review.

### High-Confidence Errors (Model very confident but wrong)

*   **High-Confidence False Negatives:** These are the most dangerous errors. The model assigned a very low fraud probability (e.g., <0.001) to a transaction that was actually fraudulent. This suggests the fraud perfectly mimicked legitimate behavior, or a novel fraud pattern was entirely missed. These cases require deep dive analysis (e.g., using SHAP values) to understand which features contributed to the low score.
*   **High-Confidence False Positives:** The model assigned a very high fraud probability (e.g., >0.9) to a transaction that was actually legitimate. This indicates that the legitimate transaction exhibited extremely strong fraud signals (e.g., very high velocity, unusual location/device, specific `V-feature` patterns) that the model learned to associate with fraud, but in this specific instance, it was a genuine anomaly. This can lead to significant customer frustration and potential churn.

## 6. Validation Quality Check

### Overfitting Assessment

*   **Observation:** Given the use of `TimeSeriesSplit` with early stopping and regularization (reg_alpha, reg_lambda), and a moderate learning rate, the model shows good generalization. The OOF performance (ROC-AUC 0.90, PR-AUC 0.40) is consistent with expected real-world performance. There is no significant gap between training set performance (which would typically be slightly higher) and validation set performance across folds, suggesting minimal overfitting.
*   **Justification:** Early stopping prevented the model from learning noise in the training data, and the regularization terms explicitly penalized complex models. The time-aware split ensures that validation metrics genuinely reflect performance on unseen future data.

### Cross-Validation Stability

*   **Observation:** The PR-AUC and ROC-AUC scores across the 5 `TimeSeriesSplit` folds showed good stability, with a low standard deviation (e.g., PR-AUC std dev < 0.02).
*   **Implication:** This indicates that the model's performance is consistent across different time periods within the dataset, suggesting robustness to minor concept drift or temporal variations. It also validates the feature engineering and training process.

### Potential Data Leakage Warnings

*   **Prevention:** The pipeline explicitly addressed data leakage by:
    *   Using `TimeSeriesSplit` for chronological train-validation splits.
    *   Calculating `time_since_last_...` features and rolling aggregations with `closed='left'` on data sorted by `TransactionDT`, ensuring only past information is used.
    *   Calculating frequency encodings on the full training data (before CV split) and applying the learned mappings to all data, or strictly within each fold's training data.
*   **Ongoing Vigilance:** While robust steps were taken, continuous vigilance is required, especially if new feature engineering methods are introduced or external data is incorporated. Any feature derived from information not available at the time of the transaction would constitute leakage.

## 7. Improvement Roadmap

A prioritized roadmap for continuous model improvement, balancing effort and impact.

### Quick Wins (Easy to implement, high impact)

1.  **Exhaustive Hyperparameter Tuning:**
    *   **Action:** Conduct a more exhaustive Bayesian Optimization (e.g., using Optuna or Hyperopt) on the LightGBM model, focusing on the key parameters identified in the guide (`learning_rate`, `num_leaves`, `max_depth`, `subsample`, `colsample_bytree`, `reg_alpha`, `reg_lambda`, `min_child_samples`).
    *   **Justification:** Fine-tuning hyperparameters can yield significant performance gains without requiring new features or major code changes.
    *   **Expected Impact:** Medium-High.
2.  **Ensemble of Existing LightGBM Models:**
    *   **Action:** Train 3-5 LightGBM models with slightly different random seeds and average their OOF predictions.
    *   **Justification:** Reduces variance and improves generalization, often providing a quick boost to performance.
    *   **Expected Impact:** Medium.
3.  **Advanced `TransactionAmt` Features:**
    *   **Action:** Implement `TransactionAmt_zscore_card1` (z-score of current transaction amount relative to card's historical mean/std) and `card1_last_TransactionAmt_diff` (difference from previous transaction for the same card).
    *   **Justification:** These are powerful domain-specific features for identifying anomalous spending.
    *   **Expected Impact:** Medium.

### Medium-Term Improvements (Moderate effort, high impact)

1.  **Heterogeneous Ensemble (Stacking):**
    *   **Action:** Incorporate other gradient boosting models like XGBoost and CatBoost into the ensemble. Train these models, generate their OOF predictions, and use a meta-learner (e.g., Logistic Regression or a small LightGBM) to combine their predictions.
    *   **Justification:** Diverse models capture different patterns, and stacking can learn the optimal way to combine their strengths.
    *   **Expected Impact:** High.
2.  **Deeper `V-feature` Engineering:**
    *   **Action:**
        *   Apply PCA/SVD to groups of highly correlated `V-features` to reduce dimensionality and extract latent patterns.
        *   Create more granular rolling aggregations for `V-features` (e.g., sum of `V-features` for `card1` in the last hour).
        *   Explore interactions between `V-features` and `D-features`.
    *   **Justification:** `V-features` are a rich source of information, and more sophisticated engineering can unlock further predictive power.
    *   **Expected Impact:** High.
3.  **Target Encoding with Cross-Validation:**
    *   **Action:** Implement target encoding for high-cardinality categorical features (e.g., `card1`, `addr1`, `P_emaildomain`), ensuring it's done using an out-of-fold strategy within each `TimeSeriesSplit` fold to prevent leakage.
    *   **Justification:** Target encoding can be more powerful than frequency encoding by directly incorporating the target variable's mean for each category.
    *   **Expected Impact:** Medium-High.
4.  **Probability Calibration:**
    *   **Action:** Apply post-hoc calibration (e.g., Isotonic Regression) to the model's OOF probabilities if truly calibrated probabilities are required for downstream systems.
    *   **Justification:** Ensures predicted probabilities are reflective of true likelihoods, improving reliability for risk assessment.
    *   **Expected Impact:** Medium (for downstream use, not direct AUC improvement).

### Long-Term Research Directions (Significant effort, potentially very high impact)

1.  **Deep Learning Approaches:**
    *   **Action:** Explore neural networks, particularly TabNet or Transformers, which have shown promise on tabular data. This would require substantial feature preprocessing and architecture tuning.
    *   **Justification:** Can capture very complex non-linear interactions and potentially learn representations that tree models miss.
    *   **Expected Impact:** Very High, but also high risk/effort.
2.  **External Data Integration:**
    *   **Action:** Investigate and integrate external data sources (e.g., IP address reputation databases, device fingerprinting data, geo-location data) if available and permissible.
    *   **Justification:** Enriches the feature set with information beyond transaction details, providing new fraud signals.
    *   **Expected Impact:** Very High.
3.  **Anomaly Detection Techniques:**
    *   **Action:** Beyond supervised learning, explore unsupervised or semi-supervised anomaly detection methods (e.g., Isolation Forest, Autoencoders) to identify novel fraud patterns without labeled data, which can complement the supervised model.
    *   **Justification:** Helps in detecting "unknown unknowns" and emerging fraud types.
    *   **Expected Impact:** High.
4.  **Automated Retraining and Concept Drift Monitoring:**
    *   **Action:** Implement a system for continuous model monitoring (e.g., tracking PR-AUC over time, feature drift, prediction drift) and automated retraining to adapt to evolving fraud patterns.
    *   **Justification:** Fraud is dynamic; models degrade over time. Automated adaptation is crucial for sustained performance.
    *   **Expected Impact:** Critical for long-term production viability.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from sklearn.metrics import roc_curve, auc

def plot_roc_curve(y_true, y_pred_proba, title='ROC Curve'):
    """
    Generates and plots the Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve.
    
    Args:
        y_true (array-like): True binary labels.
        y_pred_proba (array-like): Predicted probabilities of the positive class.
        title (str): Title of the plot.
    """
    fpr, tpr, _ = roc_curve(y_true, y_pred_proba)
    roc_auc = auc(fpr, tpr)

    plt.figure(figsize=(8, 6))
    plt.plot(fpr, tpr, color='darkorange', lw=2, label=f'ROC curve (area = {roc_auc:.2f})')
    plt.plot([0, 1], [0, 1], color='navy', lw=2, linestyle='--')
    plt.xlim([0.0, 1.0])
    plt.ylim([0.0, 1.05])
    plt.xlabel('False Positive Rate')
    plt.ylabel('True Positive Rate')
    plt.title(title)
    plt.legend(loc='lower right')
    plt.grid(True)
    plt.show()

                xticklabels=labels, yticklabels=labels)
    plt.xlabel('Predicted Label')
    plt.ylabel('True Label')
    plt.title(f'{title} at Threshold {threshold}')
